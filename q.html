Q: Which members of Congress are criticizing the National Security Agency?

Criticism of the NSA has been a bipartisan affair. On the Democratic side, the leading critics have been Sen. Ron Wyden (D-OR) and Sen. Mark Udall (D-CO). They’ve worked closely with Kentucky Republican Sen. Rand Paul. In September 2013 the three senators co-sponsored legislation that would place strict new limits on the agency’s spying powers.

In the House of Representatives, Patriot Act author James Sensenbrenner (R-WI) has become a leading critic of the Obama administration’s spying programs along with Rep. Justin Amash (R-MI). In July 2013, Amash offered an amendment to an intelligence funding bill that would have shut down the NSA’s phone records program. It was defeated, but the surprisingly close 205-217 vote was widely seen as a rebuke to the Obama administration.

Perhaps the most ambitious proposal to limit the NSA was introduced by Rep. Rush Holt (D-NJ). The Surveillance State Repeal Act he introduced in August 2013 would repeal both the Patriot Act and the FISA Amendments Act, the legal foundations of the NSA’s post-9/11 spying programs.
